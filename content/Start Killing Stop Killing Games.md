Stop Killing Games is a movement about ensuring the playability of video games in the long-term. I'm a game dev and a lover of old games. You would assume this is a cause near and dear to my heart!

Unfortunately, the execution of SKG is mostly nonsense and often exhausting to engage with. And the reason is its embrace of an extremely common pattern in "movements" especially internet movements:

> The cause is just. The solution is simple. The opposition is selfish. 

I want to break down why this pattern isolates movements, including SKG, from effective change and why it implies a different goal entirely.

## The cause is just.
In the case of Stop Killing Games it is! It's not unreasonable at all to want to be able to play the games you purchased forever. It makes sense and is easy to place in the context of other metaphors (if you buy a house you can live there forever). Most people have an applicable personal anecdote of a game they loved that's no longer playable.

I'll take the time here to make the normal disclaimer as well: Parts of Stop Killing Games are reasonable and should be done. Pulling "always on" DRM checks from single player games when the servers that respond to those requests are taken offline should be mandatory. Private servers for discontinued games, that you own, should be legal. 

There's a reason this is the first step: It's the easiest. Finding a just cause is not hard. Convincing someone your cause is just is also not hard, especially when it's something they've likely encountered personally.

#### But it's just video games
Some criticism of SKG has pointed to the cause as too small to be worth it. I don't subscribe to this at all. If you want to spend your time on a cause that you think is worthwhile, fucking go for it. That's awesome. It doesn't have to change the world. 

There's nothing wrong with advocating for longer lived video games. The problem using the reasonablness of the cause to convince people that...

## The solution is simple.
No it's fucking not. 

There's two kinds of people in the world: Those who think the solutions are simple and those who have tried to solve the problem. 

This is where rhetoric starts to appear. It usually looks like this:
- "It's just common sense."
- "You don't need to be an expert to see..."
- "If the incentives were aligned you'd see this happen immediately."

Every single one of these statements serves the exact same purpose: Take expert knowledge and put it on the same level as "layman knowledge". This is the single most important step of the process as it allows the growing audience, most of whom have no expert knowledge, to directly "participate". 

"It's just common sense, you don't need to be an expert to see it!" is always a cover for a lack of knowledge so complete that it has made jumps that an expert would know not to make in the first place. It's worth asking why the head of the movement is a youtuber and not a developer[^5].

### "Ok so explain why it's technically infeasible."

"Do you have 15-20 years?"[^2]

It's not a snippy, or dismissive, answer. The reason some topics like SKG are so tractable to this "common sense" approach is because the real answer can't be soundbited. It's hugely complex, often requiring real experience earned over a long time of already working with the problem.

We all get this on some level and for some topics. No one tries to undertand industrial engineering before driving over bridges. But then a topic comes along that hooks us anecdotally and it all goes out the window. "My favorite game *was* broken. I do hate that. *There's got to be a better way.*"

## The opposition is selfish.

"They kill games. We fight back." It's the largest and boldest (literally, it is in bold) statement on the SKG website[^1]. 

But who is "they"? And why would they **want** to kill games? What does it even mean to "kill" a game?

The reality of the answer is, of course, massively complex. It sits at the intersection of copyright law, technical constraints, internal company politics and external nation level politics. It would take dozens of people hundred of hours to untangle an even partially satisfying solution. A good advocacy group facilitates exactly that: Pulls a ton of people from a ton of disciplines together and sits them down for the long-haul.

But I can feel your eyes glazing over. This isn't what was promised with "They kill games. We fight back." You wanted change in weeks not years. And you're not a lawyer or a programmer so you're starting to feel disincluded. No, the answer can't be that it's complicated or hard to fix...it must be that the enemy is lying to you. 

**They** don't want the simple fix because they're selfish and greedy. Ah, that feels better. It isn't about complexity, it's about morality. You can handle morality.

---

This is another release valve for the audience that may lack expert knowledge. The "justness" of the cause means they're already prepared to morally judge anyone who doesn't believe it in it. It's a small step to believing that opposition isn't seeing something they don't, or weighting values differently, but rather actively immoral, greedy, selfish.

Interestingly this is one place where SKG hasn't quite nailed down the rhetoric yet. Specifically when a **developer** (as opposed to a publisher or other vaguely described "business person") pushes back against some of the movements core claims. Usually by explaining the difficulty of preserving online games. The prescribed response is to cast them as immorally motivated...but fortunately a lot of people struggle buy into that.

## Where it stands
I said earlier that "There's two kinds of people in the world: Those who think the solutions are simple and those who have tried to solve the problem." The good news here is that SKG is rapidly becoming the second type. 

...and they're getting their ass kicked. SKG will, of course, tell you this is all good news and expected. A step in the process. It's not. [They were rightfully patted on the head and told to try again.](https://www.dexerto.com/gaming/stop-killing-games-fails-to-secure-eu-law-despite-1-3m-signatures-3376431/)

Here's where I'll say the most positive thing I have to say about SKG generally, they're taking action. And people who take action go one of two directions:
1) They learn, adapt, gain knowledge, and become more effective in achieving their goals (though never as effective as their initial rhetoric) 
2) They double down on the "simple" solution to gain an audience at the cost of any real accomplishments[^4].

The next few months of SKG are going to show exactly which direction they choose.

## This isn't about SKG
Hopefully it's clear by now that this pattern isn't really about Stop Killing Games. It's about populism. **Genuine** populism but populism all the same. And in SKG, like all populist movements, change isn't the goal. Moralism and righteousness are. 

### So what can we do:
#### Listen to experts.
All opinions are not equal. "It's common sense" isn't an explanation. "You don't know enough to have a strong opinion on this" isn't a condemnation of you as a person, it is a factual statement about your current knowledge. If you don't like it, gain more knowledge (often a years long process, not a google search), ask an expert, or play inside the domains where you **do** have expert knowledge[^3].

#### Don't stop!
The cause **may** be just! There's big wins to be had here. The grand shame of populism is that it's ineffective at accomplishing things because it refuses to be realistic in favor of being moralistic. Drop the rhetoric, consult with the experts, pick some concrete asks and go get them! It won't feel as good in the moment but it will **affect change in the world**.

#### Watch for any movement that uses "Just cause. Simple solution. Selfish opponents."
It feels nice to think the world's a little simpler than it appears. It feels nice to think that "evil" and not "the yawning pit of complexity" is the cause of problems but resist that impulse! Dive in the pit! It's not quite as deep as you think and there's a lot of smart, hard-working people already in there working on filling it.
